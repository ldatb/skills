# Client health

The judgment behind the client-satisfaction steps. This reference inherits the
[foundation](../../../meta/foundation/SKILL.md) doctrine — predictability over
cleverness — and the depth bar for assessing a relationship follows the tech-lead
standard in [the review lenses](../../../engineering/code-review/references/review-lenses.md):
judge the relationship against what it must deliver, not only against the last
pleasant call. The retention mechanics here are the client-relationship cousin of
[the retention playbook](../../../marketing/growth/references/retention.md):
that reference works product-level SaaS churn signals, while this one works the
one-to-one consulting relationship a founder owns by hand.

Client satisfaction is the health of a paying relationship, measured before the
client says a word about leaving. A churned client is the lagging confirmation of a
decision the client reached weeks earlier, in silence. The leverage is **leading**,
not lagging — the satisfaction score on a feedback form arrives after the trust
already moved. This reference names the signals that precede the decision, the
cadence that surfaces them, the way expectations are set so reality clears the bar,
the recovery when reality misses, and the record that ties it all into the second
brain so the next quarter routes faster.

A founder's instinct under pressure is to go quiet and ship harder, trusting the
work to speak. Silence is the single most expensive move in a client relationship,
because an unmanaged client fills the silence with the worst available story.
Communicate first; the work is necessary, never sufficient.

## 1. The client health model

A leading signal is an observable change that predicts dissatisfaction before the
client states it. The five below carry the most predictive weight for a services or
consulting relationship. Read each as a trend against the client's own baseline,
not against a global average — a client who replied within the hour for three months
and now takes three days has moved, even though three days is fine in absolute terms.

- **Response latency.** Replies slow, calls get rescheduled, or a decision-maker who
  used to answer now routes through a deputy. Latency is the earliest tell, because
  attention withdraws before money does. A reply time that doubles against the
  account's trailing baseline over two exchanges is a standard early-warning trip.
- **Sentiment.** Tone cools in writing and on calls — shorter messages, fewer
  questions, "fine" where there used to be enthusiasm, a cc added to a thread that
  used to be one-to-one. A single terse note about a deliverable the client depends
  on outweighs ten warm notes about side topics.
- **Usage and engagement.** The client stops opening the work, skips the demo, leaves
  the shared doc untouched, or stops bringing new problems to the relationship. A
  client who has disengaged from the output has stopped converting the work into
  value, and unvalued work is unrenewed work.
- **Payment timeliness.** An invoice that always cleared in a week now sits, a renewal
  conversation gets deferred, or finance starts querying line items that never drew
  questions before. Slow payment is a confidence signal wearing an accounting costume.
- **Scope tension.** Requests drift past the agreed statement of work, "quick favors"
  accumulate, or the client and the founder describe the engagement's boundary
  differently on the same call. Scope tension predicts a blow-up, because an
  unmanaged boundary turns every future delivery into a disappointment against an
  imagined larger one.

These signals roll into one **red / yellow / green health score** per client, the
routing key for every action downstream:

- **Green.** Latency steady at baseline, warm sentiment, active engagement, payment
  on time, scope aligned. The play is to protect the steady state and harvest
  advocacy, never to interrupt a healthy client with manufactured concern.
- **Yellow.** One signal has tripped — latency doubled, tone cooled, an invoice
  slipped, or scope started to drift — with the rest intact. Yellow is the entire
  point of the model, because yellow is the window where a thirty-minute
  conversation still changes the outcome.
- **Red.** Two or more signals have tripped together, or one signal has gone severe
  (an invoice in collections, an explicit complaint, a renewal declined). Red demands
  a same-week, named-human intervention and an honest assessment of whether the
  relationship is recoverable.

Score the signals, weight them, sum, and bucket — the bucket routes the cadence and
the intervention. A relationship with no leading signals is a relationship managed by
surprise.

Red flag: a "client health" view that shows only revenue and renewal date. Both are
lagging. A relationship tracked only by what has already been billed learns of every
loss after the window to prevent it has shut.

## 2. The relationship cadence

Satisfaction is maintained on a rhythm, not rescued in a crisis. The cadence is the
machine that surfaces the signals in section 1 while they are still yellow. Three
beats cover most engagements.

- **Regular check-ins.** A short, recurring touch — weekly or biweekly — on progress,
  blockers, and what the client is hearing internally. The check-in's real job is not
  the status update; it is the standing invitation that lets a small concern surface
  before it compounds. A check-in the client starts skipping is itself a yellow signal.
- **Quarterly business reviews (QBRs).** A periodic step back from the work to the
  outcome: what the engagement set out to move, what it has moved, and where it goes
  next. The QBR is where renewal and expansion are earned, because a client shown the
  realized value against the original goal renews on evidence rather than on goodwill.
- **Proactive updates.** Unprompted notes that get ahead of news — a slip flagged
  before the deadline, a risk named before it lands, a win shared the day it happens.
  A proactive update spends a small amount of comfort now to prevent a large loss of
  trust later, and it is the cheapest trust-building move available.

Match the cadence intensity to the health score: a green client holds the standing
rhythm, a yellow client gets an added unscheduled touch aimed at the tripped signal,
and a red client moves to a direct, senior, same-week conversation. Cadence is the
delivery mechanism for everything else in this reference.

Red flag: the only contact is delivery and the invoice. A relationship that talks
only when work ships and money is due has no channel for a concern to travel down
before it becomes a cancellation.

## 3. Expectation management

Most dissatisfaction is not a quality failure; it is a gap between what the client
expected and what arrived. The gap is managed at the moment of promising, not at the
moment of delivery, so the highest-leverage satisfaction work happens before any work
ships. The governing rule is **under-promise and over-deliver**: set the bar where
reality clears it, then beat it.

- **Scope.** State what the engagement includes and, with equal clarity, what it
  excludes. An explicit out-of-scope list prevents the slow drift in section 1,
  because an unwritten boundary is read by the client as generously as the client
  wishes. A change to scope is a deliberate, recorded decision, never an absorbed
  favor.
- **Timelines.** Commit to dates with built-in margin and a named assumption set.
  A date promised at the median case breaks half the time, so a date promised with
  buffer and stated dependencies holds — and a delivery that lands early reads as
  competence rather than relief.
- **Status.** Keep the client's picture of where things stand continuously true, so
  no delivery is ever a surprise relative to the last update. A status that drifts
  from reality converts every honest delivery into a disappointment against an
  inflated expectation the client was allowed to keep.

The whole discipline reduces to one move: control the expectation at the point of the
promise so the delivery clears it. An over-promise is a satisfaction debt taken on at
the moment it is made, and the bill always arrives.

Red flag: promising the best case to win the room. A best-case promise feels generous
in the meeting and reads as a broken commitment on the delivery date, and the second
costs far more trust than the first ever bought.

## 4. Handling dissatisfaction

Dissatisfaction is inevitable; mishandled dissatisfaction is what loses clients. A
complaint surfaced early is a gift, because the alternative is the same decision
reached in silence and discovered at renewal. The recovery follows a fixed sequence,
and a client whose problem is recovered well is frequently more loyal than one who
never had a problem.

- **Surface early.** Make raising a concern cheap and expected — ask the direct
  question on every check-in, and treat a quiet client as a yellow signal rather than
  a contented one. Silence is not satisfaction; silence is the most dangerous reading
  on the board.
- **Acknowledge.** Name the problem back in the client's own terms, without defense or
  deflection, before proposing anything. A client who feels heard will accept a slower
  fix, while a client who feels argued with will reject even a fast one.
- **Remediate.** Give a concrete action and a date — the workaround now and the full
  fix when it lands — owned by a named person. A remediation without a date is not a
  remediation; it is a hope the client is asked to share.
- **Follow up.** Return after the fix to confirm it landed and the relationship
  recovered, then fold the cause back into the signals so the next occurrence is
  caught earlier. A recovery is closed by the client confirming it, not by the fix
  shipping.

Run the sequence in order, because skipping acknowledgement to jump to remediation
reads as a client being managed rather than heard. The order is the point.

Red flag: meeting a complaint with a defense of the work. The first response decides
the recovery, and a defensive first response tells the client the relationship is the
founder's ego, not the client's outcome.

## 5. Gathering feedback

A health score built only from passive signals is a guess; feedback turns the guess
into evidence. Combine a quantitative pulse with the qualitative "why" behind it,
because a number alone names the temperature without naming the cause.

- **NPS / CSAT.** A periodic one-number pulse — likelihood to recommend (NPS) or
  satisfaction with a delivery (CSAT) — tracked against the client's own trend. The
  value is the movement: a score that drops from its own prior reading is a fired
  signal even while the absolute number still looks acceptable.
- **Qualitative.** The open question that follows the number — what is working, what
  is not, what would make the engagement a clear win. A single specific sentence
  ("the weekly report saves my Monday") routes the work better than a column of tens,
  because it names the value the client actually buys.

Treat a feedback request as a relationship act, not an inbox survey: a founder asking
the question by name and acting on the answer builds more trust than the data alone
ever returns. Feedback unread, or read and unactioned, is worse than no feedback —
the ask raised an expectation the silence then broke.

Red flag: a satisfaction number with no qualitative channel beneath it. A falling
score with no "why" tells the founder the relationship is cooling and gives nothing
to act on.

## 6. Turning happy clients into referrals and case studies

A green client is an under-used asset. Satisfaction that is never converted is
goodwill left on the table, so the cadence harvests advocacy the way the save play
harvests at-risk recovery. Timing is the whole game: ask at the peak, just after a
delivered win, never cold.

- **Referrals.** A warm introduction from a satisfied client is the highest-trust
  pipeline a founder owns. Ask specifically — name the kind of person who would
  benefit — at the moment a win lands, when the value is vivid rather than remembered.
- **Case studies.** A documented outcome turns one client's result into proof for the
  next prospect. The QBR is the natural moment to propose it, because the realized
  value is already named and quantified in the room.
- **Testimonials and references.** A short quote or a willingness to take a reference
  call costs the client little at a satisfaction peak and compounds for the founder
  across every future deal.

Gate every ask on a green score and a recent win, because a referral request to a
yellow client converts a recoverable relationship into a remembered imposition. The
ask is part of the relationship; a mistimed ask is a withdrawal from it.

Red flag: never asking, or asking on a calendar instead of on a win. Advocacy asked
cold lands as a transaction, while advocacy asked at the peak lands as a favor gladly
given.

## 7. Recording it in the vault

A relationship managed from memory is a relationship that resets every time the
founder's attention moves. The record is what makes the health score a trend rather
than a feeling, so every signal, score, and intervention lands in the second brain.
Note creation, linking, and updates are delegated to
[second-brain-crud](../../../obsidian/second-brain-crud/SKILL.md) — the satisfaction
work decides *what* to record and the CRUD skill owns *how* the note is written, so
the file operation stays deterministic.

- **The client note.** One durable note per client, linked to the project notes the
  engagement delivers, the people notes for the contacts inside it, and the commitment
  notes for the promises made to it. The links are the point: a client note wired to
  projects, people, and commitments lets a slipping commitment or a quiet contact
  surface against the relationship it endangers.
- **The health log.** A dated, append-only record of the score and the signal readings
  behind it at each assessment. The log is what turns five point-in-time readings into
  the trend that fires a yellow before it reddens, and append-only means a past entry
  is preserved rather than overwritten when the next assessment lands.
- **The correlation.** Read the health log beside the linked project and commitment
  notes so a dip lines up with its cause — a missed commitment, a slipped delivery, a
  contact who left. A health score that moves without a recorded cause is a signal half
  captured.

Record the assessment as a fact, then read it against the linked notes to find the
driver. The vault is the memory that lets the next quarter's cadence route on
evidence instead of on the last thing the founder happened to recall.

Red flag: the client relationship lives in an inbox and a head. An undocumented
relationship cannot show a trend, so its first visible signal is the cancellation.

## 8. Messaging the client is an external mutation

Every assessment, score, and intervention plan in this reference is internal work the
founder owns outright. The one action that crosses the boundary is **sending a message
to the client** — a check-in note, a QBR invite, a remediation reply, a feedback
request, a referral ask. A sent message cannot be unsent, and it changes a live
relationship, so it carries the same approval discipline a destructive command carries
in code.

- **Draft, do not send.** The skill prepares the message — the check-in, the
  acknowledgement, the feedback ask — and stops at the draft. The founder reads it and
  sends it by hand.
- **Approval gates the send.** A drafted client message waits on explicit founder
  approval before any external channel carries it, because the relationship is the
  founder's to spend, not the agent's.

Red flag: an agent that sends a client message on its own judgment. A relationship is
spent one message at a time, and the founder owns every withdrawal.

## 9. Failure modes

- **Silent dissatisfaction.** Reading a quiet client as a happy one. Silence is the
  withdrawal of attention, the earliest signal there is, and the founder who waits for
  the client to complain has chosen to learn at the last possible moment.
- **Surprise churn.** A renewal declined with no prior warning on the board. Surprise
  churn is never actually a surprise; it is a leading signal that was never
  instrumented, so the decision arrived fully formed and unaddressed.
- **Scope creep unmanaged.** Absorbing "quick favors" until the engagement no longer
  resembles the agreement. Unmanaged scope erodes margin, breeds resentment on both
  sides, and resets the client's baseline expectation upward so every future delivery
  disappoints against an imagined larger one.
- **No feedback loop.** Never asking, or asking and not acting. A relationship with no
  feedback channel runs on the founder's assumptions, and assumptions about a cooling
  client are wrong in the expensive direction.
- **Over-promising.** Winning the room with the best case. An over-promise is a debt
  drawn at the moment of the promise, repaid with interest on the delivery date in the
  one currency a relationship cannot reprint — trust.

## 10. Red flags

- The client view shows only revenue and renewal date, both lagging.
- A quiet client is filed as a happy client, with no signal tracked for the silence.
- Contact happens only at delivery and at invoicing, with no channel for an early concern.
- A complaint is met first with a defense of the work, before any acknowledgement.
- Scope has drifted past the written agreement, with the favors never recorded.
- Feedback is never gathered, or gathered and never acted on.
- A client message is sent on the agent's own judgment, with no founder approval.
- The relationship lives in an inbox and a memory, so its health shows no trend.

## 11. Worked example — assessing one client's health

**Client.** A mid-sized SaaS company on a $12,000 / month fractional-CTO retainer,
seven months tenured, previously a reliable green.

**Signals.** The health score moves from green to **yellow**. Response latency from
the primary contact stretched from same-day to four days across the last two
exchanges, and the tone of the last two messages cooled — shorter, a colleague newly
cc'd on a thread that had been one-to-one. Payment is current, engagement with the
deliverables is steady, and scope is still aligned. So one strong signal (latency) and
one soft signal (sentiment) have tripped while the other three hold, which is the
definition of yellow and the exact window the model exists to catch.

**Diagnosis.** The latency and the new cc track to a likely internal shift: a contact
losing authority, gaining a skeptical stakeholder, or quietly questioning the
retainer's value ahead of a renewal still two months out. Read the health log beside
the linked project notes — the last QBR is overdue and the most recent win was never
explicitly tied back to the client's original goal. So the driver is a value-perception
gap opening in silence, not a delivery failure, which rules out the wrong moves: there
is no complaint to remediate and no scope to renegotiate yet.

**Intervention.** The right play is a **proactive value re-anchor delivered by a named
human**, ahead of the renewal rather than at it. The plan: bring the overdue QBR
forward, walk the realized outcomes against the original mandate, and surface the
recent win the client never connected to their goal. The founder also addresses the
new stakeholder directly, converting a skeptic-in-the-room into an informed one. The
agent **drafts** the QBR invitation and the value-recap note and **stops there**,
because sending a client message is an external mutation — the founder reviews both
drafts and sends them by hand under section 8.

**Measurement.** The closing metric is the signal recovering, not the QBR being held.
Track response latency back toward same-day and sentiment back to warm across the next
two exchanges after the review. A recovery toward baseline closes the yellow; a
continued slide escalates the client to red and moves the renewal conversation forward
as an explicit save.

**Record.** The yellow reading, the two tripped signals, and the diagnosed driver are
written to the health log via [second-brain-crud](../../../obsidian/second-brain-crud/SKILL.md),
the client note is linked to the renewal commitment and the new stakeholder's people
note, and the overdue-QBR cause is folded back into the cadence so a lapsed QBR fires a
signal on its own next quarter. The vault now carries the trend, so the next dip routes
on evidence rather than on memory.
