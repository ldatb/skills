# People management — the depth bar

Managing people is the highest-leverage, lowest-feedback work a tech lead or founder does. A
shipped feature tells you within days whether it worked; a management choice tells you in
quarters, through attrition, stalled growth, or a surprise resignation. The discipline that
closes that feedback gap is the same one this repository prizes for code: record the signal
when it happens, correlate it over time, and act on a written record instead of memory.

This reference sets the bar for *what good looks like*. The procedure in
[SKILL.md](../SKILL.md) is the order of operations; the note mechanics belong to the
second-brain-crud skill at [../../../obsidian/second-brain-crud/SKILL.md](../../../obsidian/second-brain-crud/SKILL.md).
Decide *what* to record here; let that skill perform the write.

A standing constraint runs through everything below: **people data is sensitive**. Mark
sensitivity in frontmatter, summarize private detail rather than transcribing it, and keep
medical, financial, legal, and personal-hardship specifics out of the vault in favor of a
neutral pointer ("discussed a personal matter; revisit on the agreed date").

---

## 1. The 1:1 system

The 1:1 is the load-bearing ritual of management. A team without 1:1s manages by
interruption and review-season ambush. A team with good 1:1s surfaces problems while they are
still small and cheap.

**Regular cadence.** Hold the 1:1 weekly for most reports, fortnightly for senior or remote
reports who prefer depth over frequency. Protect the slot: a 1:1 that gets cancelled whenever
something "more important" appears tells the report they are the lowest priority on the
calendar. Reschedule rather than skip.

**The report owns the agenda.** A 1:1 is the report's meeting, not a status report for the
manager. Status belongs in the tracker; the 1:1 is for what the tracker cannot show —
blockers, ambiguity, frustration, ambition, career questions. Open with the report's topics.
A shared, running agenda note that both parties append to between sessions removes the "what
should we talk about" cold open.

**Listen more than talk.** Target a listening ratio well above half. The manager's job in the
session is to ask, draw out, and unblock, not to fill the air. Silence after a question is a
tool: the most useful sentence a report says often arrives after the pause the manager was
tempted to fill.

**Track themes over time.** A single 1:1 is a data point; the value compounds across the
series. Keep dated logs and re-read the last few before each session. A concern raised lightly
three weeks running is a pattern, not a mood — and only a written record reveals it. Themes
worth tracking: recurring blockers, shifts in energy or engagement, stated career direction,
and commitments either side made.

## 2. Goals and growth

**Clear expectations first.** Ambiguity about what "good" means is the most common and most
corrosive management failure. Each report should be able to state, without prompting, what
their role is responsible for and what success looks like this cycle. Write expectations down;
spoken expectations drift and then get disputed.

**A real growth plan.** Growth is the manager's product. A growth plan names where the report
wants to go, the capabilities that path demands, and the next concrete step toward it. Review
it quarterly, not annually. A report with no growth path is a future resignation already in
motion — strong people leave stagnation long before they leave pay.

**Stretch versus comfort.** Calibrate the mix deliberately. Pure comfort-zone work is
pleasant and produces no growth; pure stretch is exhausting and produces burnout and visible
failure. Aim for assignments that are mostly within reach with a deliberate edge of the
unfamiliar — hard enough to grow, safe enough to recover from a stumble. Name which strengths
a task leans on and which edge it stretches, so the stretch is a choice rather than an
accident.

## 3. Feedback

Feedback is the cheapest tool in management and the most hoarded. The instinct to save it for
the review is the single most damaging habit on this list.

**Timely.** Give feedback close to the event, while the details are fresh and the behavior is
still changeable. A month-old correction lands as an ambush; same-day recognition lands as
truth.

**Specific.** Vague praise ("great job") and vague criticism ("be more proactive") are both
useless because neither tells the report what to repeat or change. Anchor every piece of
feedback to an observable event.

**Behavior, not person.** Address what the report *did*, never who they *are*. "The PR went
out without tests" is actionable; "you're careless" is an attack on identity that invites
defense, not change.

### SBI — Situation, Behavior, Impact

The SBI model forces the specificity that good feedback needs, in three parts:

- **Situation** — the concrete when and where, so context is shared. *"In yesterday's
  incident review…"*
- **Behavior** — the observable action, stripped of interpretation. *"…you walked through the
  timeline and named your own mistake first…"*
- **Impact** — the effect it had, on the work, the team, or you. *"…which made it safe for two
  others to admit theirs, and we found the real root cause faster."*

SBI works identically for recognition and for correction. Recognition told in SBI form
teaches the report exactly what to repeat; correction told in SBI form keeps the conversation
on a fixable action rather than a character verdict.

**Balance recognition and correction.** A manager who only corrects trains the team to dread
their attention. A manager who only praises is noise. Recognition is not sugar-coating — name
strengths because repeating them is the fastest route to performance. Authenticity matters
more than any ratio: forced praise stapled to every criticism reads as a formula and erodes
trust.

## 4. Performance management

**Continuous, not annual.** The annual review should contain zero surprises. A review that
reveals a problem the report is hearing for the first time is a documentation failure by the
manager, not a performance failure by the report. The verdict at review time should be a
summary of feedback already given, not breaking news.

**Document examples as they happen.** Memory is lossy and recency-biased; by review season a
manager recalls last month and forgets the quarter. Log concrete examples — strong and weak —
in the moment, dated and specific. A handful of dated examples turns a contested, feelings-led
review into a grounded conversation about a visible record.

**Calibrate against the role, not against other people.** Judge performance against the
written expectations for the level, not against the loudest or most visible peer. Visibility
and impact are different variables, and confusing them rewards self-promotion over
contribution.

## 5. Delegation

Delegation is how a manager scales and how a report grows. Done badly, delegation is either
abdication (handing off without context) or micromanagement (handing off without authority).

**Match the task to the level.** Calibrate what you delegate to where the report is. A stretch
delegation to a junior needs a tighter frame and more checkpoints; the same task to a senior
needs the goal and the constraints, then room. A task far above someone's current level,
dropped without scaffolding, sets them up to fail in public.

**Context, not control.** Hand over the *why* and the *what* — the goal, the constraints, the
definition of done, the deadline — and leave the *how* to the report. Prescribing the method
removes the learning and signals distrust. Delegate outcomes and decision rights, not
keystrokes. The test of a delegation: the report can make the small calls without returning to
ask, because they hold the context to make them well.

## 6. Psychological safety and trust

Psychological safety is the belief that speaking up — a question, a concern, a mistake, a
dissent — will not be punished. The construct is well supported by Amy Edmondson's research
and by Google's Project Aristotle, which found it the strongest single predictor of effective
teams. Safety is the substrate the rest of this reference runs on: feedback, hard 1:1
conversations, and honest performance talk all require a report who trusts the manager enough
to be candid.

Trust is built in small, consistent acts and destroyed in single large ones. The mechanics
that build it:

- **Admit your own mistakes first.** A manager who never errs publicly teaches the team to
  hide errors.
- **Respond to bad news as a gift.** The reaction to the first hard truth sets whether a
  second one ever arrives.
- **Keep confidences.** A confidence repeated is trust ended, permanently.
- **Be consistent.** Predictable beats nice. A report can work with a demanding manager whose
  reactions are stable; an erratic manager is unworkable at any level of kindness.

Safety is not the absence of standards. The strongest teams pair high safety with high
expectations — candor about a high bar, held without fear.

## 7. Handling underperformance

Underperformance is where weak management compounds into a crisis. The pattern that works is
**early, clear, documented, supportive — then decisive**.

1. **Early.** Name the gap at the first sign, not at review season. The cost of delay is
   borne by the team carrying the slack and by the report denied the chance to correct while
   correction is still cheap.
2. **Clear.** State the specific gap and the specific change required, in plain words. Hinting
   is a kindness that reads as cruelty in hindsight, because the report never got a fair shot
   to fix what was never named.
3. **Documented.** Record the conversation, the gap, the agreed plan, and the review date,
   dated and factual. Documentation protects the report (a fair, written record), the manager,
   and the company.
4. **Supportive.** Pair the hard message with concrete help — clearer goals, pairing,
   training, a manageable next milestone. Most underperformance is a fixable mismatch of
   expectations, skills, or context, not a character flaw.
5. **Decisive.** Where genuine support over a fair, bounded period does not move the needle,
   act. Carrying sustained underperformance indefinitely is unfair to the team that
   compensates for it and to the report living in limbo. A formal plan, by this point, is a
   documented continuation of an honest conversation already held — never the opening move.

A formal performance plan must never be the report's first awareness of a problem. A
surprise plan is a documentation and feedback failure made visible, and it reliably ends in
exit plus resentment rather than recovery.

## 8. Recording it in the vault

The record is what separates managing on evidence from managing on vibes. The shape that
correlates well over time:

- **One person note per report**, the hub. Frontmatter carries role, level, start date, 1:1
  cadence, and a sensitivity marker. Body holds expectations, the current growth plan, and
  links out to the rest.
- **1:1 logs**, one dated note per session, linked from the person note. Agenda (report's
  items first), themes, decisions, and next actions with owners.
- **Feedback notes**, one per instance, in SBI form, dated and linked.
- **Links to projects** the report touches, so contribution and load are visible alongside the
  person.

Correlation is the payoff: with 1:1 logs, feedback notes, and project links converging on one
person note, a quarterly pattern that no single session revealed becomes legible at a glance.

**Sensitivity discipline.** Mark private notes in frontmatter (for example
`sensitivity: private`). Summarize rather than transcribe: record *that* a hardship was
discussed and the agreed follow-up, not the intimate detail. Keep health, legal, financial,
and family specifics out of the vault. When in doubt, write the neutral pointer and hold the
detail in your head.

## 9. Failure modes

Each item below is a common, named way management quietly fails. Treat the presence of one as
a finding to fix.

- **Skipped 1:1s.** The slot exists on the calendar and gets cancelled whenever delivery
  pressure rises. The signal that should have surfaced a problem early never fires.
- **Feedback hoarded for review time.** Observations saved up for the annual review, where
  they land months late as a batch ambush — stale, unfixable, and unfair.
- **Vague expectations.** "Be more senior" with no definition. The report cannot hit a target
  they were never shown, and the gap becomes a dispute instead of a plan.
- **Surprise PIPs.** A formal plan as the first signal of a problem. A near-guaranteed exit
  dressed up as a development tool.
- **No growth path.** Reports parked in steady-state delivery with no next step. Strong people
  read the ceiling and leave; weak retention masquerades as stability.
- **Undocumented issues.** Problems discussed verbally and never written down. By review or
  exit, two irreconcilable memories and no record to arbitrate.

## 10. Red flags

Signals that demand attention now, not at the next scheduled checkpoint:

- A report goes quiet in 1:1s after previously engaging — withdrawal often precedes
  disengagement or departure.
- Cancelled or perpetually rescheduled 1:1s, from either side.
- A report consistently surprised by feedback — evidence the loop is not running.
- A manager who cannot name, from memory, what each report is working toward.
- A high performer whose growth conversations have gone silent.
- Performance concerns that exist only in the manager's head, with nothing dated on the
  record.
- A team that never disagrees with the manager — typically low safety, not high alignment.
- A formal plan being drafted for a problem never raised informally first.

## 11. Worked example — an SBI feedback note

A feedback note records a single instance in SBI form, dated and linked, with sensitivity
marked. The body separates situation, behavior, and impact so the record stays specific and
re-readable.

```markdown
---
type: feedback
person: "[[people/jordan-lee]]"
date: 2026-06-18
kind: recognition        # recognition | correction
sensitivity: private
tags: [feedback, sbi, 1on1]
---

# Feedback — Jordan Lee — 2026-06-18

**Situation.** During Tuesday's payments incident review.

**Behavior.** Jordan walked the team through the failure timeline and named their
own misconfiguration as the trigger before anyone asked, then proposed the guardrail
to prevent a recurrence.

**Impact.** Two other engineers then volunteered their own contributing mistakes,
the review reached the real root cause faster, and the blameless tone held for the
whole session.

**Delivered:** in person, same day, 2026-06-18.
**Follow-up:** none required — reinforce the pattern at the next 1:1.

> Sensitivity: private. Summary only; no personnel detail beyond the work event.
```

What makes the note work: the three parts are distinct (no interpretation smuggled into
Behavior), the impact is concrete (who did what differently), delivery is dated and close to
the event, and sensitivity is marked. A correction note takes the identical shape — the only
change is `kind: correction` and an Impact that names the cost rather than the benefit, plus a
required, dated follow-up.

## 12. Worked example — a 1:1 record

A 1:1 log is one dated note per session, linked from the person note, with the report's agenda
first and explicit owners on every action. Private threads are summarized, not transcribed.

```markdown
---
type: 1on1
person: "[[people/jordan-lee]]"
date: 2026-06-20
cadence: weekly
sensitivity: private
tags: [1on1, log]
links:
  - "[[projects/payments-reliability]]"
  - "[[feedback/jordan-lee-2026-06-18]]"
---

# 1:1 — Jordan Lee — 2026-06-20

## Report's agenda
- Wants more architectural ownership on the payments work.
- Frustrated by slow review turnaround blocking their PRs.
- Personal matter affecting hours next week (summary only).

## Discussion & themes
- Architectural ownership: ready for it. Agreed Jordan drafts the retry-logic
  design doc and drives review. (Theme: growth toward senior — third week raised.)
- Review latency: a real, recurring blocker, not a one-off. Root cause is a single
  overloaded reviewer.

## Decisions
- Jordan owns the retry-logic design doc end to end.
- Add a second reviewer to the payments rotation to relieve the bottleneck.

## Next actions
- [ ] Jordan: draft retry-logic design doc — by 2026-06-27.
- [ ] Manager: add second reviewer to rotation — by 2026-06-23.
- [ ] Manager: revisit the personal matter at next 1:1, per Jordan's preference.

## Carry-over themes
- Growth toward senior: raised three weeks running — open a formal growth-plan
  update.

> Sensitivity: private. Personal matter recorded as a neutral pointer only.
```

What makes the log work: the report's agenda leads, themes are tracked across sessions (the
"three weeks running" note converts a mood into a pattern), every action has a named owner and
a date, the note links to the project and the related feedback so correlation is one hop away,
and the private thread is a pointer rather than a transcript.

---

## The bar, in one line

Manage on a written record, not memory: steady 1:1s the report owns, feedback given in SBI
while it is cheap, growth on a real path, trouble named early, and every signal recorded —
sensitively — in the vault so patterns become legible before they become crises.
