---
name: copy-editing
description: Tighten and sharpen existing marketing or product copy without changing its meaning — cut filler, hedges, and throat-clearing; prefer active voice; hold the author's tone. Use when the user asks to edit, tighten, polish, or copy-edit a headline, landing page, email, ad, or product description, or says copy feels bloated, wordy, or weak.
---

Edit copy the way a line editor does: cut what does not earn its place, sharpen what stays, and leave the author's argument and voice intact. The job is subtraction and precision, not rewriting — a reader who knew the draft should recognize the edit as the same piece, only tighter.

Run the passes in order. An earlier pass changes the text a later pass reads, so order is load-bearing: fix what the copy *says* before polishing how it *sounds*.

## Steps

1. **Anchor the meaning.** Before cutting a word, write one sentence naming the claim, the audience, and the call to action the copy must carry. This anchor is the contract; an edit that breaks it is a rewrite, not an edit. The step is done when the anchor sentence exists and names all three.

2. **Pass for clarity.** Read each sentence and name the one idea it carries. Split any sentence carrying two ideas; replace jargon a non-expert would stumble on with the plain term. The step is done when every sentence carries exactly one idea and no undefined jargon remains.

3. **Pass for concision.** Apply the [editing passes](references/editing-passes.md) cut list: filler words, hedges, throat-clearing openers, and nominalizations. Convert passive voice to active where the actor is known and the active reads stronger. The step is done when no filler word from the cut list remains and the draft is shorter.

4. **Pass for flow.** Read the copy aloud in your head and mark every spot the rhythm stalls. Vary sentence length around each stall and fix the transitions between ideas. The step is done when no marked stall survives and the reading order matches the argument order.

5. **Pass for voice and terminology.** Compare the edit's tone against the anchor draft, then list every product or feature name used. The step is done when the tone matches the original register and one term names each concept throughout.

6. **Verify against the anchor.** Read the edit beside the anchor sentence from step 1. Confirm the claim, audience, and call to action survive unchanged and the meaning is identical. The step is done when the edit satisfies the anchor and a diff shows only cuts and sharpening, no new claims.

7. **Report the edit.** Return the tightened copy, the percent of words cut, and a short list of what changed by pass. The step is done when the deliverable states the word-count delta and names each substantive change.
