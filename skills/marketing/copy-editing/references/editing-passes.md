# Editing passes

Copy editing is **subtraction and precision applied to an existing draft**, not
composition. The author already chose the claim, the audience, and the voice. The
editor's leverage is removing what dilutes that choice and sharpening what carries it
— while leaving the meaning untouched. A reader who knew the draft should recognize the
result as the same piece, only tighter and sharper.

The passes are ordered by dependency, not by taste. An earlier pass rewrites the text a
later pass will read, so running them out of order wastes work: polishing the rhythm of
a sentence you are about to cut is effort spent on a corpse. Fix what the copy *says*
(clarity), then how much it spends saying it (concision), then how it *moves*
(flow), then how it *sounds* (voice), then whether a fact slipped (correctness).

## Pass 1 — Clarity

The highest-weight pass. Copy that reads smoothly but leaves the reader unsure what is
being offered has failed, however elegant the prose.

- Does each sentence carry exactly one idea? A sentence with two ideas hides one of them.
- Would a reader outside the company understand every term? Jargon and acronyms are
  clarity debt the reader pays.
- Is the subject of each sentence the thing the reader cares about, not an abstraction?
- Does the first line earn the second? A reader who stalls on the opener never reaches
  the offer.

Red flags: a sentence you must re-read to parse; an acronym with no expansion on first
use; a subject that is a process noun rather than a person or product; a claim buried in
the third clause of a compound sentence.

## Pass 2 — Concision

Cut the words that occupy space without adding meaning. Concision is not brevity for its
own sake — a precise long sentence beats a vague short one. The target is *density*:
every word earns its place.

### Filler words

Delete words that add length, not meaning. Common offenders: *very*, *really*, *just*,
*quite*, *actually*, *basically*, *simply*, *literally*, *of course*, *in order to*
(use *to*), *the fact that* (use *that*), *at this point in time* (use *now*).

### Hedges

Hedges drain authority from a claim. Delete or commit: *we believe*, *we think*,
*arguably*, *somewhat*, *fairly*, *kind of*, *sort of*, *it could be argued that*,
*in many cases*, *generally speaking*. Marketing copy that hedges asks the reader to
supply the confidence the writer withheld.

### Throat-clearing openers

A throat-clearing phrase delays the sentence's actual subject. Cut the windup and start
on the verb or the noun that matters: *It is important to note that*, *What this means
is that*, *There is / There are* (name the subject), *When it comes to X*, *In today's
world*, *Needless to say*.

### Nominalizations

A nominalization buries a verb inside an abstract noun, and the buried verb is almost
always stronger. Restore the verb:

- *provide a recommendation* becomes *recommend*
- *make a decision* becomes *decide*
- *perform an analysis of* becomes *analyze*
- *give consideration to* becomes *consider*
- *achieve a reduction in* becomes *reduce*
- *is reflective of* becomes *reflects*

### Passive where active is stronger

Convert passive to active when the actor is known and the active reading lands harder.
*Mistakes were made* hides the actor; *the system retries failed jobs* names one. Keep
the passive only in two cases: the actor is genuinely unknown, or the object deserves the
emphasis (*your data is encrypted at rest* keeps the reader's data in the subject slot,
which is the point).

Red flags: two adverbs stacked before one verb; a sentence that survives intact after you
delete its first four words; *of* chained three times (*the optimization of the
allocation of resources*); a passive whose hidden actor is the product itself.

## Pass 3 — Flow

Once the words are right and the fat is gone, fix how the copy moves.

- **Rhythm.** Read it aloud in your head. Uniform sentence length lulls; a stall signals
  a sentence to break or rebalance. Vary length on purpose — a short sentence after two
  long ones lands like a punch.
- **Transitions.** Each idea should follow from the last. A reader who feels a jump is
  reading an argument with a missing rung.
- **Order.** The reading order should match the argument order: hook, value, proof,
  call to action. A proof point before the value it supports arrives unmotivated.

Red flags: five sentences of identical length and shape; an idea that arrives with no
bridge from the prior one; a call to action stranded before the reader is sold; a list
whose items do not share a grammatical shape (broken parallelism).

## Pass 4 — Voice consistency

The voice belongs to the author and the brand. The editor's duty is to *preserve* it,
not overwrite it with a house style or a blander register.

- Does the edited register match the draft — same level of formality, same warmth, same
  swagger or restraint?
- Did a cut accidentally flatten a deliberate stylistic choice (a fragment for emphasis,
  a rhetorical question, a brand-specific turn of phrase)?
- Is one term used for one concept throughout? Switching between *users*, *customers*,
  and *members* for the same group makes the reader hunt for a distinction that is not
  there.

Red flags: an edit that reads like a different writer; a punchy brand line sanded into a
generic one; the same concept under three different names; an em dash aside deleted
because it looked informal when informality was the point.

## Pass 5 — Correctness

Last, because correctness checks survive only on text that is otherwise final.

- Are product names, feature names, prices, dates, and figures exactly right? A typo in a
  price or a product name is a credibility wound.
- Do claims still hold after the cuts? Trimming a qualifier can turn a true bounded claim
  into a false absolute one.
- Spelling, grammar, punctuation — the table stakes a reader notices only when broken.

Red flags: a number or date you did not verify against the source; an absolute claim
(*always*, *never*, *the only*) created by deleting a qualifier; an inconsistent product
name between the headline and the body; a dangling modifier left by a sentence merge.

## Readability targets

Targets, not laws. A deliberate violation that serves the copy beats blind compliance.

- **Sentence length:** average around 15–20 words; let a few run shorter for punch.
- **Paragraph length:** 2–4 sentences for web and email; a single-sentence paragraph is
  a legitimate emphasis tool.
- **Reading grade:** aim for grade 7–9 for general marketing copy; a technical audience
  tolerates higher, a broad consumer audience wants lower.
- **Word choice:** prefer the shorter, more concrete word when meaning is equal — *use*
  over *utilize*, *help* over *facilitate*, *buy* over *purchase*.
- **Active-voice share:** the majority of sentences should be active; reserve the passive
  for the two cases named in Pass 2.

## Failure modes

The three ways a copy edit destroys value instead of adding it:

1. **Over-editing into blandness.** Cutting past the fat into the muscle. The voice goes
   flat, the personality drains out, and the copy reads like every other landing page.
   The fix: stop when the meaning is clear and the draft is tight — a distinctive line is
   not a defect to sand smooth.
2. **Changing the meaning.** An edit that alters the claim, drops a load-bearing
   qualifier, or shifts the call to action is a rewrite wearing an edit's clothes. The
   anchor sentence (claim, audience, call to action) is the guardrail; check the edit
   against it before shipping.
3. **Inconsistent terminology.** Introducing a synonym for a term the draft already
   settled. Two words for one concept reads as two concepts. Pick the draft's word and
   hold it.

A fourth, quieter failure: editing to a personal style the brand never asked for.
Preference is not improvement.

## Red flags (skill-level)

Stop and reconsider the whole edit when:

- The edit no longer satisfies the anchor sentence — the claim, audience, or call to
  action moved.
- A diff against the draft shows additions of substance, not just deletions and swaps.
- The word count barely moved and no sentence got sharper — the pass did nothing.
- You renamed a product or feature, or changed a price, date, or figure.
- The result reads in a voice the author would not recognize.

## Worked example — shrinking a bloated paragraph

A product-page paragraph, before:

> In today's fast-paced digital world, it is important to note that our innovative
> platform basically provides users with the ability to perform the management of all of
> their various tasks in one single centralized location. We really believe that, in many
> cases, this can help to facilitate a significant improvement in overall team
> productivity, and it is our hope that teams will find it to be quite useful for their
> day-to-day collaboration needs.

That is 78 words. Walking the passes:

- **Clarity:** two ideas tangled together — *what the platform does* and *what it claims
  to deliver*. Split them.
- **Concision:** cut throat-clearing (*In today's fast-paced digital world*, *it is
  important to note that*), filler (*basically*, *really*, *quite*, *single*, *various*,
  *overall*), and hedges (*We really believe*, *in many cases*, *it is our hope that*).
  Restore buried verbs: *provides the ability to perform the management of* becomes
  *manage*; *facilitate a significant improvement in* becomes *boost*.
- **Voice:** the draft is warm and confident underneath the padding; the edit keeps that
  register rather than going clinical.

After:

> Our platform puts every task in one place. Teams manage their work from a single
> hub — and collaborate faster because of it.

That is 24 words, a 69% cut. The claim (*one place for tasks*), the audience (*teams*),
and the implied benefit (*faster collaboration*) all survive; only the padding is gone.
Even a more conservative edit that keeps a third sentence of detail lands near a 40% cut
while preserving every load-bearing word — the bloat, not the meaning, is what leaves.
