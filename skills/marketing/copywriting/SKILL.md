Marketing copy earns attention, then a decision. A draft converts when one reader recognizes one problem, believes one claim, and takes one action — so the work is subtraction and proof, not adjectives. Persuasion is a procedure, not a mood: name the reader, pick the frame, draft, cut to one idea, back every claim, sharpen the ask.

Push the work down a ladder before reaching for prose: a stated reader and offer remove guesswork, a named framework removes blank-page drift, a proof inventory removes unsupported hype. The model writes only the irreducibly creative line — the headline turn, the benefit phrasing — after the structure is fixed.

## Steps

1. **Fix reader, offer, and action.** Write one sentence each: who the reader is and the pain they feel right now, what the offer is and the one transformation it delivers, and the single action this copy must produce (buy, sign up, book, reply). The step is done when all three are written and the action is one verb, not a menu.

2. **Pick the framework.** Choose one structure from [the frameworks reference](references/copywriting-frameworks.md) — AIDA, PAS, BAB, or the 4 Ps — and write one line saying why it fits this reader's awareness and this offer. The step is done when one framework is named with its rationale, not two hedged together.

3. **Draft against the framework's beats.** Write the copy section by section, following the chosen framework's beats and the headline patterns in the reference, leading with the reader's problem or desired outcome rather than the product. The step is done when every beat of the framework has copy and the opening names a reader problem or outcome, not a feature.

4. **Cut to the rule of one.** Reduce the draft to one reader, one core idea, and one action; delete every second idea, hedging qualifier, and sentence that does not move the reader toward the action. The step is done when removing any remaining sentence would weaken the single argument.

5. **Convert features to benefits and add proof.** Rewrite each feature as the outcome the reader gets, then attach a specific proof to every claim — a number, a named result, a testimonial, a guarantee — per the proof and specificity rules in the reference. The step is done when no claim stands without evidence and no vague intensifier ("best", "powerful", "seamless") survives without a concrete fact behind it.

6. **Sharpen the CTA and run the red-flags pass.** Rewrite the call-to-action as a specific, first-person, friction-named ask, then check the draft against the failure modes and red-flags checklist in the reference. The step is done when the CTA states exactly what happens next and zero red flags remain.

7. **Read it aloud against voice.** Read the final copy aloud once for the founder's voice and rhythm, flattening any line that sounds like a corporate brochure rather than a person. The step is done when every sentence sounds like the founder speaking and no jargon filler remains.
