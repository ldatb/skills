# Copywriting frameworks

Persuasive copy is not decoration on a product page. A reader gives copy three seconds
of attention and one decision. Copy that converts moves a specific person from their
current state to one action by naming a real problem, promising one outcome, and proving
the claim. Everything below is the craft of doing that on purpose instead of by feel.

This reference is the depth behind the SKILL.md procedure. The procedure tells you *what
order* to work in; this page tells you *how to judge* each move and *what good looks
like*. Read it when drafting a hero, a sales page, a product description, or a CTA — and
when a draft reads vague, feature-dumped, or hype-heavy and you need to know why.

## The job of copy

Three things in sequence, every time:

1. **Earn attention.** The headline buys the first line; the first line buys the second.
   A reader who is not stopped is never persuaded.
2. **Earn belief.** A claim without proof is noise. Specificity and evidence convert
   attention into trust.
3. **Earn the decision.** One action, stated plainly, with the friction named and lowered.

A draft that does only the first is hype. A draft that does only the second is a spec
sheet. A draft that does only the third is a button no one clicks. The frameworks below
exist to keep all three in one piece of copy, in the right order.

## The main frameworks and when each fits

A framework is a running order for the reader's attention. The wrong framework forces
the reader through beats they do not need; the right one matches where their awareness
already is. Pick by **reader awareness** — how much the reader already knows about their
problem and your solution.

### AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action

The general-purpose arc. Hook attention, build interest with relevance, stoke desire with
the outcome, then ask for the action.

- **Fits:** cold or lightly-aware readers; top-of-funnel landing pages, ads, broad hero
  sections where you cannot assume the reader knows they have the problem.
- **Beats:** Attention (headline that stops the scroll) → Interest (why this is relevant
  to *them*) → Desire (the transformed state, made vivid and proven) → Action (one CTA).
- **Fails when:** the reader is already problem-aware and AIDA's slow build wastes their
  time. Use PAS instead.

### PAS — Problem, Agitate, Solve

Name the pain, twist the knife, then relieve it. The most reliable framework for selling
because it leads with the reader's reality, not the product.

- **Fits:** problem-aware readers; sales pages, email, any copy where the reader already
  feels the pain and you need them to feel its cost before they will move.
- **Beats:** Problem (state the pain in the reader's own words) → Agitate (the cost of
  leaving it unsolved — time, money, embarrassment, missed outcome) → Solve (your offer
  as the specific relief, with proof).
- **Fails when:** agitation tips into manipulation or invented pain. Agitate a *real*
  consequence the reader already suspects; never manufacture fear.

### BAB — Before, After, Bridge

Paint the reader's world now, paint it transformed, then position the offer as the bridge
between them.

- **Fits:** transformation-driven offers; products whose value is a clear state change
  (before: drowning in X; after: X handled). Strong for founders selling an outcome.
- **Beats:** Before (the painful status quo) → After (the desired world, concrete and
  proven) → Bridge (the offer as the only step from one to the other).
- **Fails when:** the "after" is generic ("save time and money"). The after must be a
  specific, visualizable outcome or the bridge has nothing to span.

### The 4 Ps — Promise, Picture, Proof, Push

Lead with a bold promise, make the reader picture it, prove it can be delivered, then
push for action. Proof is a first-class beat, not an afterthought.

- **Fits:** claim-heavy or skeptical markets; longer sales pages, anywhere trust is the
  bottleneck and a single testimonial will not carry the weight.
- **Beats:** Promise (the headline outcome) → Picture (the reader living it) → Proof
  (data, testimonials, demonstrations, guarantee) → Push (urgency + CTA).
- **Fails when:** the promise outruns the proof. The bolder the promise, the heavier the
  proof beat must be, or the whole page reads as hype.

### Picking in one move

| Reader state | Best fit | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Doesn't know they have the problem | AIDA | Earns attention before assuming pain |
| Feels the pain, weighing options | PAS | Leads with their reality, raises the cost |
| Wants a clear transformation | BAB | Frames offer as the bridge to the after |
| Skeptical, needs convincing | 4 Ps | Makes proof a structural beat |

When two seem to fit, choose the one whose *first beat* matches what the reader is
already thinking. A problem-aware reader opened with AIDA's attention beat feels stalled;
the same reader opened with PAS's problem beat feels understood.

## Headline patterns

The headline is 80% of the work — most readers read it and nothing else. A headline does
one job: earn the next line. Patterns that earn it:

- **Outcome promise:** the specific result the reader wants. *"Ship your SaaS landing
  page this weekend."* Strong because it names a concrete, time-bound outcome.
- **How-to / mechanism:** *"How to write a hero section that converts cold traffic."*
  Strong for problem-aware readers searching for a method.
- **Question that names the pain:** *"Still losing signups on a hero no one reads?"*
  Strong because the reader answers "yes" internally and keeps reading.
- **Number / specificity:** *"The 6-line hero that doubled our trial signups."* Specific
  numbers read as true; round-number superlatives read as marketing.
- **Objection reversal:** *"You don't need a designer to launch a page that sells."*
  Strong when a known objection is the reader's first thought.
- **"Without" framing:** *"Get a high-converting page — without hiring an agency."*
  Names the desired outcome and removes the feared cost in one line.

A headline fails when it is clever instead of clear, when it describes the *product*
instead of the reader's *outcome*, or when it could sit on any competitor's page. Test:
could a reader who has never seen the product tell what they get and why it matters? If
not, it is a label, not a headline.

## The rule of one

The single highest-leverage discipline in copywriting. Every piece of copy serves:

- **One reader.** Write to one specific person, not a segment. "Founders selling a SaaS
  through their own audience" beats "businesses." Copy aimed at everyone persuades no one,
  because no one feels it was written for them.
- **One idea.** One core promise per page. A second big idea does not add persuasion — a
  second idea splits attention and halves the force of the first. Supporting points serve
  the one idea and introduce no new ones.
- **One action.** One CTA, repeated, never competing. "Start your free trial" *and* "Book
  a demo" *and* "Read the docs" is three doors; a reader facing three doors walks through
  none. Pick the one action this copy exists to produce.

The rule of one is a *cutting* tool. After drafting, run one test — remove a sentence,
and if the single argument is unharmed, the sentence was serving a second idea, so cut it.
Most weak copy is not under-written; weak copy is two or three pieces fighting in one.

## Features vs. benefits

A feature is what the product *is*. A benefit is what the reader *gets*. Readers buy
benefits; they tolerate features only as proof the benefit is real.

| Feature (what it is) | Benefit (what they get) |
|---|---|
| "256-bit encryption" | "Your customers' data stays private — and you stay out of the headlines" |
| "Drag-and-drop editor" | "Launch a page today, without waiting on a developer" |
| "Automated weekly reports" | "Know what's working on Monday morning, without pulling the data yourself" |
| "10GB storage" | "Never delete a project to make room" |

The translation move: take a feature and ask **"so what?"** until you reach an outcome
the reader feels. "Drag-and-drop" → so what? → "no developer needed" → so what? → "launch
today instead of next sprint." Stop at the outcome that matches the reader's pain.

Features are not banned — they earn their place as *proof of the benefit*, stated after
the benefit, not instead of it. "Launch today, no developer needed — the editor is pure
drag-and-drop." Benefit leads; feature substantiates.

## Proof and specificity

A claim is a liability until it is proven. Specificity *is* proof — the more concrete a
statement, the more true it reads. Sources of proof, strongest first for a founder brand:

- **Specific results / numbers.** "Cut our onboarding from 9 minutes to 90 seconds"
  beats "faster onboarding." Exact figures read as measured; round numbers read as
  guessed.
- **Testimonials with attribution.** A named customer with a specific outcome ("'We
  closed 3 deals in the first week.' — Maria, agency owner") beats an anonymous "Great
  product!" Specific, attributed, outcome-focused, or it is wallpaper.
- **Demonstration.** Show the thing working — a screenshot, a before/after, a live
  number. Showing beats claiming.
- **The founder's own story.** For a personal brand, "I built this because I lost a month
  to X" is proof of understanding the problem. Authentic narrative is a proof asset most
  companies cannot use.
- **Risk reversal.** A guarantee ("30 days, full refund, no questions") proves confidence
  and removes the reader's risk in one move.

Specificity discipline: replace every vague intensifier with a fact. "Powerful" → what
does it do? "Trusted by many" → how many, who? "Fast" → how fast, versus what? If a word
could appear on any competitor's page, it is carrying no weight — cut it or back it.

## Voice and tone

Voice is the founder's personality made consistent; tone is how it flexes per context.
For a personal brand, voice *is* the moat — readers buy from a person they recognize, not
a faceless company.

- **Write like the founder talks.** Read the copy aloud, line by line. A line the founder
  would never say at a dinner table ("leverage synergies to maximize value") does not
  belong on the page. Contractions, short sentences, and a real point of view beat polish.
- **Match tone to the moment.** A headline can be bold; a guarantee should be plain and
  reassuring; an error message should be calm. Same voice, adjusted temperature.
- **Specific beats clever.** Wit that obscures the offer costs a conversion. Be vivid in
  service of clarity, never instead of it.
- **Second person, active voice.** "You ship faster" beats "Users experience improved
  velocity." Address the reader directly; let verbs do the work.

## Failure modes

The four ways marketing copy reliably dies. Each is a specific, recurring defect — learn
to name them on sight.

- **Vague claims.** "Best-in-class solution that empowers your business." Says nothing,
  proves nothing, fits any product. *Fix:* replace each abstraction with a specific
  outcome and a fact.
- **Feature-dumping.** A list of what the product *has*, with no translation to what the
  reader *gets*. The reader has to do the work of imagining the benefit, and won't. *Fix:*
  lead with the benefit; demote features to proof.
- **Weak CTA.** "Submit", "Learn more", "Click here" — generic, friction-blind, asking
  for effort with no payoff named. *Fix:* state the specific next outcome and lower the
  perceived cost ("Start free — no card required").
- **Hype without proof.** Big promises, exclamation marks, superlatives, zero evidence.
  Hype reads as desperation and triggers the reader's skepticism. *Fix:* match each degree
  of boldness in the promise with a degree of proof, or lower the promise.

## Red flags

A fast checklist to run against any draft before it ships. Each flag is a likely
conversion leak:

- The headline describes the **product**, not the reader's **outcome**.
- More than **one** call-to-action competes for the click.
- A **claim** appears with no number, testimonial, demonstration, or guarantee behind it.
- A **feature** is listed with no "so what" benefit attached.
- A vague intensifier ("powerful", "seamless", "world-class", "best") stands with no fact.
- The copy could sit on a **competitor's** page unchanged — nothing is specific to this
  offer or this founder.
- The CTA names an **effort** ("Submit", "Sign up") but no **payoff** and no friction
  removed.
- The draft carries **two** big ideas; the rule of one is broken.
- A sentence sounds like a **brochure**, not the founder — corporate voice has crept in.
- Read aloud, the copy **bores** the writer; the same copy will bore the reader faster.

## Worked example: weak hero → strong hero

A landing-page hero (headline + subhead + CTA) for a founder selling a course that
teaches indie developers to write their own marketing copy.

### Before (weak)

> # Welcome to CopyCraft Pro
> The ultimate all-in-one solution to supercharge your marketing and take your
> business to the next level. Our powerful platform leverages proven strategies to
> help you succeed.
>
> [ Learn More ]

What is wrong, by lens:

- **Headline:** "Welcome to CopyCraft Pro" is a label, not a promise. It describes the
  product and names no outcome. A reader learns nothing they want.
- **Rule of one:** "marketing", "business", "next level", "proven strategies", "succeed"
  — several vague ideas, no single argument. No specific reader is addressed.
- **Features vs. benefits:** "all-in-one solution", "powerful platform" describe the
  product, not what the reader gets. The reader must guess the benefit.
- **Proof / specificity:** "ultimate", "supercharge", "powerful", "proven" — every word a
  vague intensifier, not one fact. Pure hype, zero evidence.
- **CTA:** "Learn More" names an effort and no payoff; friction-blind.
- **Voice:** corporate brochure; no human, no founder, no point of view.

### After (strong) — PAS, because the reader is problem-aware

> # Your product is great. Your landing page is killing it.
> You can build the thing — but the copy reads like a spec sheet, and visitors leave
> without signing up. In one weekend, learn the exact framework I used to take my own
> SaaS from a 1.2% to a 4.6% signup rate — no marketing degree, no agency.
>
> [ Start the free first lesson — no card required ]
>
> *"Rewrote my hero on Sunday. Signups doubled by Friday." — Devon, indie SaaS founder*

Why it converts, by lens:

- **Headline (PAS problem beat + objection reversal):** names the reader's exact pain (a
  good product undersold by its page) and creates tension. Speaks to one reader — an indie
  developer who can build but not sell.
- **Subhead (agitate → solve):** agitates the real cost ("visitors leave without signing
  up"), then resolves it with a specific, proven mechanism ("1.2% to 4.6%", "one
  weekend") and reverses the two likely objections ("no marketing degree, no agency").
- **Features → benefits:** no feature dump; the offer is framed entirely as the outcome
  the reader gets (a page that converts).
- **Proof / specificity:** exact before/after numbers, a time bound, the founder's own
  result, and a named, outcome-specific testimonial. Every claim is backed.
- **Rule of one:** one reader, one idea (your page is leaving signups on the table, and
  here's the fix), one action.
- **CTA:** specific next step *and* friction removed ("free first lesson — no card").
- **Voice:** first person, plain, opinionated — a founder talking, not a brand
  announcing.

The move in one sentence: **stop announcing the product and start naming the reader's
problem, then prove you can solve it.**
