# Growth reference: finding growth — generation and prioritization

Growth ideas are cheap; the scarce resource is the founder's time to build and run
them. So the work is not "think of something clever" — the work is to generate many
candidates against a stated goal, then rank them honestly so the few that ship are the
few most likely to move the metric. Generation and selection are two different
cognitive modes, and collapsing them is the most common way a founder ends up busy
and flat. This reference separates the two, then arms each.

The job has a spine: a growth **thesis** — one sentence naming how this business is
supposed to grow — sits above every idea. An idea that does not serve the thesis is
not a small idea; the off-thesis idea is a distraction wearing the costume of
progress. Generate wide, but score against the thesis and the metric, never against
novelty. This reference covers the generation frameworks, divergent-then-convergent
discipline, the ICE and RICE prioritization models, how to score without lying to
yourself, the failure modes, the red flags, and a worked example.

## The growth thesis comes first

Before any list, name the thesis: the single mechanism this business bets on to
acquire and keep customers. Examples: "Content compounds — we rank for the problems
our buyers search, and organic traffic converts to trials." Or: "Every active user
invites a teammate, so the product grows itself." Or: "Sales-led — outbound books
demos, demos close, and expansion does the rest."

The thesis is load-bearing for three reasons. The thesis sets the **bar an idea must
clear** (does this feed the mechanism?). The thesis sets the **metric** every idea is
scored against (traffic, invites sent, demos booked). And the thesis kills
**shiny-channel syndrome** at the source: a viral TikTok idea is off-thesis for a
sales-led B2B tool, however fun, and naming the thesis makes that obvious before the
time is spent.

A thesis is stated well when one sentence names the mechanism and the one metric that
mechanism moves. A thesis is stated badly when it lists three channels and reads as
"we'll try everything."

## Generation frameworks

These frameworks are prompts for divergence — each is a different lens that surfaces
ideas the others miss. Run several; do not stop at the first that produces a few. The
goal of this phase is **volume**, deferring all judgment.

### The channel list and Bullseye

The channel list (from Weinberg and Mares, *Traction*) is the canonical menu of ways
to reach customers — nineteen channels, used as a checklist so no avenue is forgotten
by default: viral marketing, PR, unconventional PR, SEM (paid search), social and
display ads, offline ads, SEO, content marketing, email marketing, engineering as
marketing (free tools, calculators, widgets), targeting blogs, business development,
sales, affiliate programs, existing platforms (app stores, marketplaces), trade
shows, offline events, speaking engagements, and community building. Walk the menu and
force at least one idea per channel that is even plausibly relevant, then discard the
implausible ones during convergence — the menu's value is catching the channel the
founder would never have thought to try.

**Bullseye** is the selection method that pairs with the menu: sort channels into
three rings — the *inner* ring (the few channels worth a real test now), the *middle*
ring (possible, worth a cheap probe), and the *outer* ring (unlikely, parked). Most
founders over-invest in one familiar channel and ignore the inner-ring candidate that
would actually work, so Bullseye forces a deliberate spread of cheap tests before
budget commits to one.

### Growth loops vs funnels

A **funnel** is linear: a cohort enters at the top (an ad, a post), some fraction
converts down each stage, and the cohort exits at the bottom. Funnel thinking buys one
customer per unit of input and stops; growth becomes a treadmill where spend must rise
to keep volume flat.

A **growth loop** is a cycle whose output re-enters as its own input, so each turn
seeds the next. The canonical shapes: the *viral loop* (a user invites users who
invite users), the *content loop* (users generate content that ranks and attracts new
users who generate more — user-generated pages, reviews, programmatic SEO), and the
*paid loop* (revenue from acquired customers funds the next batch of acquisition,
which only compounds when lifetime value exceeds acquisition cost with margin). The
generative question is sharper than "how do we get more customers?" — the loop asks
"what does a new customer produce that brings the next one?" Loop ideas usually
out-rank funnel ideas on a long horizon because a loop compounds while a funnel only
spends, so tag the mode of each idea. This loop-vs-funnel distinction is the seam back
to the rest of growth: a funnel idea is measured by [the funnel
metrics](funnel-metrics.md), and a loop only compounds when [retention](retention.md)
holds.

### Jobs-to-be-done angles

The jobs-to-be-done lens reframes the product around the progress the customer is
trying to make, not the features the product happens to have. The customer "hires" a
product to do a job in a context — Clay Christensen's milkshake: commuters hired a
thick morning milkshake to make a boring drive bearable with one free hand, a job no
flavor tweak would have revealed. A distinct job, paired with the context that
triggers the job, is a marketing angle: a different pain to name in the hook, a
different moment to target, a different competitor to displace (the real competitor is
whatever the customer hires today, often a spreadsheet or doing nothing). Listing the
jobs and their trigger moments generates angles that feature-led brainstorming never
reaches.

### Competitor teardown

Pull three to five competitors and dissect their go-to-market: the channels they
visibly run (ad libraries, content cadence, where they post), the angles and pain
points their copy leads with, the keywords they rank for, the audience they target,
and — most useful — the **gaps**. A gap is a job no competitor addresses, an audience
segment everyone ignores, a channel no rival has claimed, or an angle the category has
gone numb to and a contrarian could break. The teardown generates ideas in two ways:
copy what visibly works (proven demand) and attack what no one has claimed
(uncontested ground).

### 10x not 10%

Most brainstormed ideas are incremental — a 10% tweak to an existing tactic (a better
subject line, a new ad variant). A few ideas are **10x**: a different order of
magnitude in reach or leverage (a free tool that ranks forever vs one more blog post;
a partnership that exposes the product to a partner's whole list vs one more ad). The
10x demand is a deliberate generation constraint, not a value judgment on the
incremental — force two or three ideas that would 10x a metric if they worked, because
the incremental ideas arrive on their own and the 10x ideas only arrive when demanded.
The 10x idea is usually riskier and lower-confidence — that is fine; the scoring model
in the next section reconciles a high-upside, low-confidence idea against a safe, small
one.

## Divergent then convergent

Idea work has two modes, and the two must not run at once:

- **Divergent (generation).** Produce quantity. Defer all judgment — no scoring, no
  "that won't work," no editing. The aim is a long, messy list, because the best idea
  is rarely the first and is often unlocked by a bad idea next to it. Target a concrete
  count (for instance, 15–25 raw ideas) so the mode does not exit early. Run the
  frameworks above as separate prompts to force breadth.
- **Convergent (selection).** Now judge. Cluster the raw list, cut duplicates and
  off-thesis ideas, score what remains, and pick. Quality lives here.

Collapsing the two — judging each idea as it is born — is the single most common
generation failure: the inner critic strangles the volume that makes the list good,
and the founder converges on the obvious before the non-obvious has a chance to appear.
Diverge first to a quota, then converge.

## Clustering

Between divergence and scoring, group the raw list. Many "separate" ideas are one idea
in different clothes (five content ideas are one content bet); clustering collapses
them so the scoring compares real alternatives, not near-duplicates. Cluster by
channel or by growth mechanism, name each cluster, drop ideas that serve no thesis,
and carry the strongest representative of each cluster into scoring. A good clustering
turns 20 raw ideas into 6–8 distinct bets worth ranking.

## Prioritization: ICE and RICE

A ranked list without a model is just the founder's mood ordered top to bottom.
Scoring forces the trade-offs into the open and makes the ranking defensible.

### ICE

**ICE** scores each idea on three factors, typically 1–10, then ranks by their product
(or mean — pick one and hold it constant):

- **Impact** — how much this moves the target metric when it works.
- **Confidence** — how sure you are it will work, given evidence (data, prior results,
  a comparable case) rather than hope.
- **Ease** — how cheap and fast it is to build and run; high ease means low effort.

`ICE = Impact x Confidence x Ease`. The product spreads the scores so a clear winner
separates from the pack; ranking by the sum compresses them and is weaker. ICE is fast
and fits a founder with little data — its cost is subjectivity, which the
honest-scoring rules below exist to contain.

### RICE

**RICE** adds reach and makes effort explicit, which suits a longer list or a team:

- **Reach** — how many people this affects in a set period (actual count: visitors,
  signups, users per month).
- **Impact** — effect per person reached, on a fixed scale (for example 3 = massive,
  2 = high, 1 = medium, 0.5 = low, 0.25 = minimal).
- **Confidence** — a percentage (100% / 80% / 50%) that discounts for how much the
  Reach and Impact estimates are guesses.
- **Effort** — person-time to ship, in person-months or person-weeks.

`RICE = (Reach x Impact x Confidence) / Effort`. RICE is more rigorous because Reach
and Effort are grounded in real units, but the model needs numbers a brand-new effort
may not have. Use ICE early for speed; graduate to RICE once real reach and effort data
exist. Whichever model is chosen, apply the *same* one to every idea in the round —
mixing scales makes the ranking meaningless.

### Scoring honestly

The failure of every scoring model is the same: the founder inflates the factors for
the idea already loved and the ranking just launders a pre-made decision. Guard against
it.

- **Anchor every score to evidence.** Confidence is not a vibe — a 9 demands a prior
  result, a comparable case, or data; absent evidence, confidence is low by default.
- **Make ease and effort honest.** Estimate the *whole* cost including setup, learning
  curve, and maintenance, not the optimistic build time. Founders systematically
  underestimate effort.
- **Beware the loved idea.** When the favorite tops the ranking, re-score its
  confidence against evidence specifically; the bias hides in an inflated confidence
  number.
- **Score blind, then discuss.** Assign the numbers before debating the ranking, so the
  score drives the order rather than the order back-justifying the score.
- **The model informs, it does not rule.** A score is a structured prior, not an
  oracle; a deliberate override is allowed, but the override must be stated as a reason,
  not smuggled in by quietly nudging a number.

## Failure modes

Four failure modes account for most growth effort that produces motion without
results. Name and check each.

- **Chasing tactics without a thesis.** A pile of disconnected tactics — a TikTok here,
  a webinar there — with no mechanism tying them together. Each may "work" in isolation
  and none compounds, because there is no loop or thesis for them to feed. Fix: state
  the thesis first and discard every idea that does not serve it.
- **Shiny-channel syndrome.** Jumping to whatever channel is trending (the new
  platform, the viral format) regardless of whether the buyer is there or the channel
  fits the thesis. Novelty masquerades as strategy. Fix: score the trendy channel on
  the same ICE/RICE scale as the boring one and let the number decide — a trend with
  low confidence for this audience ranks where it belongs.
- **No prioritization.** Generating a long list and then attempting all of it, or
  picking by enthusiasm, which spreads the founder's time so thin that nothing gets a
  real test. Fix: score, rank, and commit to the top few — a prioritized list whose
  tail is explicitly *not now*.
- **Ideas with no success metric.** Shipping an idea with no definition of what success
  looks like, so the result can never be judged and the idea earns neither a scale-up
  nor a kill. Fix: attach one quantified metric and a threshold to each idea before it
  ships, so the test returns a verdict instead of a vibe.

## Red flags

Each red flag below predicts wasted effort. Treat any one as a blocker until resolved.

- No growth thesis is stated, so ideas are judged on novelty rather than fit.
- The list was generated and scored in one pass, so divergence never happened.
- Every idea is a 10% tweak and no idea would 10x a metric.
- Confidence scores are high across the board with no evidence cited anywhere.
- Effort and ease estimates ignore setup, learning curve, and maintenance.
- The favorite idea tops the ranking and its confidence was never challenged.
- A picked idea has no quantified success metric or threshold.
- Trendy channels rank high purely on novelty, off-thesis, unscored against the rest.
- Twenty raw ideas were never clustered, so near-duplicates crowd out real
  alternatives.

## Worked example: eight ideas for one product, top three scored with ICE

**Product.** A founder runs "InboxZero," a $19/month email-triage SaaS for solo
consultants drowning in client email. **Thesis:** content compounds — rank for the
email-overwhelm problems consultants search, convert organic readers to trials.
**Target metric:** monthly trial signups. **Awareness:** problem-aware buyers who feel
the overload and search for relief.

**Divergent pass — eight raw ideas** (judgment deferred), each tagged with the
framework that surfaced it and its growth mode:

1. SEO content hub on "consultant email management" long-tail queries. *(Content loop;
   channel: SEO + content marketing.)*
2. A free "Inbox Overwhelm Score" calculator that ranks and gets shared. *(Engineering
   as marketing + content loop; a 10x lever.)*
3. Cold outbound to consultants on LinkedIn. *(Channel: sales; funnel.)*
4. Referral incentive — invite a peer, both get a free month. *(Viral loop.)*
5. Sponsor three consultant-audience newsletters. *(Channel: email / targeting blogs;
   funnel.)*
6. TikTok productivity skits. *(Shiny-channel candidate; channel: social.)*
7. Partnership with a consultant-coaching community for a member offer. *(Channel:
   business development; a 10x lever.)*
8. Programmatic SEO: a templated page per email-client + workflow combination.
   *(Content loop; JTBD angle — one page per job-context.)*

**Convergent pass — cluster and cut.** Idea 6 (TikTok) is off-thesis for a content/SEO
bet aimed at problem-aware searchers and gets parked — scored low rather than deleted,
so the decision stays visible. Ideas 1 and 8 cluster as "organic search"; the
programmatic play (8) is the stronger representative and carries forward. That leaves a
clean set; the three highest-potential bets go to ICE.

**Score the top three (ICE, 1–10, ranked by product):**

| Idea | Impact | Confidence | Ease | ICE | Reasoning |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Free "Overwhelm Score" tool (2) | 9 | 5 | 4 | 180 | High impact and on-thesis (ranks + shares), but confidence is a 5, not higher — the founder has no prior tool launch as evidence, and ease is 4 because it needs real build and maintenance. |
| Programmatic SEO pages (8) | 8 | 7 | 5 | 280 | Confidence 7: SEO is the stated thesis and the keyword demand is verifiable in a keyword tool, so this rests on evidence; ease 5 because the page template is one build that scales. Tops the ranking. |
| Referral free-month (4) | 6 | 6 | 7 | 252 | Lower impact (a viral loop is weak when the base is small), but ease 7 — a few days to ship — and confidence 6 from referral being a proven pattern. Second by a hair. |

**Pick and metric.** Programmatic SEO leads on a defensible confidence anchored to real
keyword data, with the referral loop a close, cheap second worth running in parallel.
The free tool is high-upside but its low confidence and high effort park it behind a
cheaper proof. **Success metric per pick:** programmatic SEO — *50 organic trial
signups/month within 90 days of indexing*; referral — *15% of active users send at
least one invite within 60 days*. Each pick now carries one named lever, one honest
score, and one quantified threshold — the shape this stage produces every run, handed
to the funnel to prove whether the bet paid.
