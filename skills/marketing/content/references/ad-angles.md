# Ad creative — angles, hooks, and formats

Paid-ad creative is not copywriting in a vacuum. A paid ad earns attention from a
scrolling stranger, frames the offer through one sharp emotional or logical lever,
and survives a platform whose native job is to keep the user scrolling past it. The
creative does the heavy lifting; targeting only decides who gets the chance to ignore
it. So the unit of work is the **angle** — the single reason this person should care
right now — expressed through a **hook** that lands in the first three seconds.

This reference covers message-market-match, the angle taxonomy, hook patterns, format
fit per platform, the test, the failure modes, the red flags, and a worked example. The
same hook-retain-reward spine drives the durable organic system in
[the content-system reference](content-system.md), and the live-trend equivalent of this
hook taxonomy lives in [the viral-research reference](viral-research.md).

## Decision procedure

1. **Define the audience and the offer**: product, price, the one audience this run
   targets, the felt pain or desire, and the awareness stage.
2. **Pick 3 to 5 distinct angles** from the eight-lever taxonomy so the set tests
   different psychology against the one audience.
3. **Write a 3-second hook per angle** using a named pattern.
4. **Match one format per angle** from the format-fit table, sized for Meta or X.
5. **Spec each concept**: visual direction, beat-by-beat script or slide sequence,
   on-screen text, and one call-to-action.
6. **Plan the test**: the constant (one audience), the varied variable (the angle), the
   budget split, and the win metric (3-second hold rate or click-through).
7. **Screen against the failure modes** and clear every red flag.

## Message-market-match

Product-market fit decides whether the offer is wanted. Message-market-match decides
whether the wanted offer gets recognized in the feed. A great product behind a flat
message dies on impression; a sharp message in front of the wrong audience burns
budget on people who will never buy. Match has three layers, and the creative fails on
the weakest one:

- **Awareness stage** (Eugene Schwartz's five stages: unaware, problem-aware,
  solution-aware, product-aware, most-aware). A product-aware reader wants a reason to
  pick *you*; a problem-aware reader does not yet know solutions exist and needs the
  problem named first. The same hook cannot serve both stages.
- **Sophistication** — how many competing claims this market has already heard. The
  first "lose weight" claim wins on the claim alone; the fifth must out-mechanism or
  out-prove the prior four, because the bare claim is now wallpaper.
- **Felt pain or desire** — the lever the angle pulls. The message names a tension the
  reader already carries, so recognition does the persuading before the argument starts.

A concept passes match review when one named angle maps to one named awareness stage
for one named audience. A concept fails when those three are blurred into "everyone who
might like this."

## The angle taxonomy

An angle is the lens the offer is viewed through — eight recurring levers, each pulling
a different emotion or logic. The list below names the lever, the emotional driver, and
the reader the lever fits.

1. **Pain** — names the problem and the cost of leaving it unsolved. Driver: relief.
   Fits the problem-aware reader who feels the symptom but has not acted.
2. **Desire** — paints the aspirational end-state the product delivers. Driver:
   wanting. Fits the reader who already accepts the goal and wants the shortcut.
3. **Curiosity** — opens an information gap the reader must close by watching or
   clicking. Driver: tension. Fits a cold, distracted feed, but the payoff must be real.
4. **Social proof** — shows that peers, crowds, or reviewers already chose this.
   Driver: belonging and safety. Fits the skeptical reader who fears being first.
5. **Authority** — borrows the credibility of an expert, a credential, or a result.
   Driver: trust. Fits a high-consideration or high-price purchase.
6. **Contrarian** — attacks a belief the market holds, then resolves the tension with
   the product. Driver: pattern-break. Fits a sophisticated market numb to the usual claims.
7. **Us-vs-them** — frames a shared enemy (a bad incumbent, a tired category norm) and
   positions the product as the side the reader belongs on. Driver: identity. Fits a
   reader with an existing grievance the brand can name.
8. **Transformation** — shows the before-and-after arc of a person like the reader.
   Driver: self-projection. Fits a reader who needs proof the change is achievable.

Angles are not mutually exclusive — a transformation ad often carries social proof —
but each concept leads with **one** dominant lever, so the test can read a clean signal.

## Hook patterns for the first three seconds

The hook is the first frame, first line, or first image. The scroll is the default; the
hook's only job is to buy the next three seconds. Eight patterns earn that pause:

- **Pattern interrupt** — a visual or claim that breaks the expected feed rhythm
  ("Stop scrolling if you have [problem]").
- **Direct call-out** — names the audience so the right reader self-selects
  ("Founders selling on Meta: read this").
- **Problem-agitate** — states the pain in the reader's own words in frame one.
- **Bold claim** — a specific, almost-too-strong promise tied to a number.
- **Curiosity gap** — withholds the payoff the reader must stay to get.
- **Question hook** — asks the exact question the reader is already silently asking.
- **Result-first** — opens on the outcome ("$0 to $40k in 90 days"), then earns it.
- **Story cold-open** — drops the viewer mid-scene with no setup, so the gap pulls them in.

A hook works when a stranger in the target audience would stop on frame one without the
sound on and without the brand name. A hook fails when it needs context the scroller
does not yet have.

## Format fit per platform

The angle is constant; the format is how the angle is built for a specific surface. The
wrong format wastes a strong angle on a surface that rejects its shape.

| Format | Best-fit angles | Platform fit | The job of frame one |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Static image** | Bold claim, social proof, contrarian | Meta feed, X timeline | One legible headline + one visual; readable while muted and thumb-stopping |
| **Carousel** | Transformation, authority, us-vs-them | Meta (Instagram, Facebook) | Slide one is the hook; each swipe advances one argument beat |
| **Short video / Reels** | Curiosity, pain, story, transformation | Meta Reels, vertical video | First 3s = hook spoken and shown; captions on; 9:16 |
| **UGC (creator-style)** | Social proof, authority, transformation | Meta and X, native to both | Looks like a person talking to camera, not a brand ad; trust from rawness |

Platform-specific notes:

- **Meta (Instagram / Facebook)** rewards native, sound-optional, mobile-first creative;
  polished "TV ad" production reads as an ad and gets skipped. Reels favors vertical
  video with on-screen captions. The feed favors a static or carousel that survives muted.
- **X** favors text-forward static images and short, punchy video tied to a strong
  written hook in the post copy itself; the post text is part of the creative, not a label.

A format passes review when the lead angle and the chosen format reinforce each other.
A format fails when a slow-build transformation arc is jammed into a single static frame,
or a one-line bold claim is stretched across a ten-slide carousel.

## The test

A paid concept is a hypothesis about which lever moves this audience, so the launch is a
controlled test, not a single bet. Hold one audience constant and vary the angle across
3 to 5 concepts, so a clean result names the winning lever rather than a tangle of
changes. Split the budget across the concepts, name the primary metric (3-second hold
rate for the hook, click-through for the offer), and let the platform find the winner. A
flat result on one angle is data about that angle, not a verdict on the product. The
winner gets fresh variations on its lever; the losers get retired.

## Failure modes

Four failure modes account for most dead creative. Name and check each before shipping.

- **Clever-but-unclear.** The ad wins a pun or a slick visual and loses the message; the
  scroller enjoys it for half a second and still has no idea what is sold or why to care.
  Clarity beats cleverness at the speed of a thumb. Fix: state the offer and the lever in
  plain words first, then add wit only where wit does not cost comprehension.
- **No hook.** The creative buries the lever behind a logo, a slow intro, or three
  seconds of b-roll; the scroll wins before the point arrives. Fix: move the sharpest
  claim or image to frame one and cut everything before it.
- **Single-angle testing.** One angle ships, underperforms, and the verdict becomes "ads
  do not work" — when only one of eight levers was ever tried. A flat result on one angle
  is data about that angle, not about the product. Fix: launch 3 to 5 distinct
  angles against the same audience and let the platform find the winner.
- **Ignoring the platform.** A landscape TV-style spot is dropped into vertical Reels, or
  a brand-voice press release is posted as X creative; the format fights the surface. Fix:
  build the creative in the surface's native shape (aspect ratio, captions, sound-optional).

## Red flags

Each red flag below predicts a weak or wasteful concept. Treat any one as a blocker until
resolved.

- The hook needs the brand name or prior context to make sense.
- One concept tries to carry three angles at once, so no single lever reads clearly.
- The same hook is reused across every awareness stage with no change.
- The creative only works with sound on, yet runs on a sound-optional surface.
- The first frame is a logo, a title card, or a slow establishing shot.
- The script argues the feature set and never names a felt pain or desire.
- The plan ships one ad and calls the channel tested.
- Production polish is high while message clarity is low (clever-but-unclear).

## Worked example: three distinct angles for one product

**Product.** A founder sells a $39/month iOS app, "FocusLock," that blocks distracting
apps on a schedule. Audience: solo founders and indie makers who lose hours to phone
distraction. Awareness: problem-aware (they feel the lost time, they have tried and
abandoned willpower). Platform: Meta Reels plus a static variant for the feed.

The three concepts below pull three different levers against that same audience, so the
test reads three clean signals rather than one muddy one.

**Angle A — Pain (lever: relief).**
- Hook (problem-agitate, frame one): "You opened your phone to check one thing — 47
  minutes ago."
- Format: short video / Reels, 9:16, captions on, creator talking to camera.
- Beat structure: name the lost hours -> the willpower that already failed -> FocusLock
  blocks the apps on a schedule so the choice is removed -> CTA.

**Angle B — Transformation (lever: self-projection).**
- Hook (result-first, frame one): "I shipped my whole app in the 3 hours a day my phone
  used to eat."
- Format: carousel (Meta), slide one is the before/after, each slide advances the arc.
- Beat structure: the distracted before -> the one change -> the focused after with a
  concrete output -> proof screenshot -> CTA.

**Angle C — Contrarian (lever: pattern-break).**
- Hook (bold contrarian claim, frame one): "Productivity apps are why you get nothing
  done. They add features. This one takes them away."
- Format: static image (X and Meta feed), one legible headline over one stark visual.
- Beat structure: attack the bloated-app norm -> reframe subtraction as the fix ->
  FocusLock as the subtraction -> CTA in post copy.

Each concept names one lever, one hook pattern, one format, and one awareness stage —
the shape this job produces every run. The concepts ship as a controlled test: one
audience held constant, the angle varied, the budget split, the winner decided by hold
rate and click-through.
