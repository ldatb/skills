# Sales enablement assets

A seller loses deals on three things: the value was not obvious, the proof was
missing, or the objection in the buyer's head went unanswered. Every asset here
exists to remove one of those. The discipline is constant — lead with the buyer's
outcome, attach proof to each claim, and pre-answer the objection before the
buyer raises it. This reference is the depth behind the sales-asset lane of the
outreach skill.

These assets serve a founder selling a product or service, where the founder is
often the seller. The same structures hand cleanly to a first sales hire later.
A sales asset is a draft until the user approves it; this skill writes the asset
and stops there.

## The asset taxonomy — what each is, and where it fires

The funnel has four moments, and a different asset clears the blocker at each.

### 1. One-pager (awareness → first meeting)
A single page a prospect reads in under a minute, or a rep leaves behind after a
cold intro. Structure: outcome headline, the problem in the buyer's words, three
value pillars (each one outcome plus one proof point), one proof strip (logos or
a single metric), one call to action. The blocker it clears: "I do not yet know
why this is worth a meeting." Failure tell: a feature list with a logo wall and
no outcome.

### 2. Pitch narrative (evaluation → champion buy-in)
The spine of a pitch deck or a live demo — a story, not a feature tour. The arc:
the world has changed (a shift the buyer feels) → the stakes (the cost of
standing still) → the promised land (the buyer's outcome) → the path (how the
product gets them there) → proof (a customer who already arrived). The blocker:
"I cannot picture the outcome, or sell it internally." Failure tell: opening on
company history or the product menu instead of the buyer's changed world.

### 3. Battlecard (evaluation → competitive bake-off)
A one-screen internal cheat sheet a rep uses live against a named competitor.
Never sent to the buyer. Structure below. The blocker: "the buyer is comparing us
to a specific alternative and the rep is improvising." Failure tell: a generic
feature matrix that fits any competitor and helps against none.

### 4. Objection-handling guide (any stage → unblock)
The scripted responses to the recurring blockers, so the answer is rehearsed
rather than invented under pressure. Framework and the five common objections
below. The blocker: "a predictable concern stalls the deal." Failure tell:
defensiveness, or a discount offered the moment price is mentioned.

### 5. Case study and testimonial (justification → trust)
Third-party proof that the outcome is real for someone like the buyer. Structures
below. The blocker: "I believe the pitch but not the promise." Failure tell: a
vanity quote with no metric and no named source.

### 6. ROI / value model (justification → budget sign-off)
The numbers a champion forwards to a budget holder. Framing below. The blocker:
"I am sold, but I have to justify the spend to someone who is not." Failure tell:
a price with no value on the other side of the scale.

Match the asset to the blocker first; the format follows from the blocker, never
the reverse.

## The objection-handling framework

Run an objection through four moves, in order. Skipping a move is the most common
reason a response falls flat.

1. **Acknowledge.** Validate the concern as legitimate so the buyer feels heard,
   not handled. One sentence, no "but."
2. **Reframe.** Shift the frame from the buyer's narrow worry to the dimension
   that actually matters — cost to value, price to risk, time to opportunity
   cost.
3. **Evidence.** Land a specific proof point on the reframed dimension: a
   customer result, a number, a comparison. Generic reassurance fails here.
4. **Confirm.** Close with a question that checks the concern is resolved and
   moves to the next step. The confirming question is what separates handling
   from arguing.

### The five common objections

**Price — "it's too expensive."** Reframe from cost to value or to
cost-of-inaction. Evidence: ROI multiple, payback period, or the price of the
problem left unsolved. Confirm: "would the case look different against the cost
of the problem itself?"

**Timing — "not right now / next quarter."** Reframe from the calendar to the
compounding cost of waiting. Evidence: what a comparable buyer lost by delaying,
or the value accrued from an early start. Confirm: "what would need to be true
for the timing to work?"

**Trust — "we've never heard of you / you're early."** Reframe from vendor size
to outcome and risk-reversal. Evidence: a named customer in the buyer's segment,
a guarantee, a pilot, or founder access. Confirm: "would a scoped pilot lower the
risk to a level you're comfortable with?"

**Status quo — "we're fine with what we have / we'll build it ourselves."**
Reframe from change-cost to the hidden cost of the current path. Evidence: the
true total cost of the workaround, or the maintenance burden of a self-build.
Confirm: "is the current approach actually free once the team's time is counted?"

**Competitor — "we're also looking at [rival]."** Reframe from feature parity to
the dimension where the product wins and the buyer's use case lands. Evidence: a
head-to-head proof point on that dimension, never a smear. Confirm: "which of
these dimensions matters most for your situation?"

## Battlecard structure

One screen, scannable mid-call. Keep it specific to one named competitor.

- **Their pitch in one line** — how the competitor positions, stated fairly.
- **Where we win** — two or three dimensions, each with a concrete proof point,
  not an adjective.
- **Where they win** — the honest gaps, so the rep is never caught off guard.
- **Landmines** — questions to plant that expose the competitor's real weakness
  for this buyer.
- **Trap-handling** — crisp answers to the competitor's strongest attacks on us.
- **Proof** — one customer who switched, with the result.

A battlecard that names no competitor and carries no proof is a feature matrix,
and it loses.

## Proof assets

### Case study structure
Problem → solution → result, with the result quantified and the customer named
(or tightly described where a name is withheld).

- **Customer** — who they are and why they resemble the buyer. Resemblance is
  what makes the proof transfer.
- **Problem** — the pain before, in their words, with a cost attached.
- **Solution** — what was deployed, in one or two sentences. The product is the
  supporting actor here, not the lead.
- **Result** — the outcome, with a number and a timeframe ("cut onboarding from 6
  weeks to 4 days").
- **Quote** — a named human, in plain speech, confirming the result.

### Testimonial structure
A short quote that names a specific outcome, attributed to a real, identified
person with role and company. A quote without a metric and without a source is
decoration; the credible one says what changed and who is staking their name on
it.

## ROI and value framing

Put value on the scale opposite the price; a price quoted alone always reads as
too high.

- **Quantify the problem first.** State the current cost — hours lost, revenue
  leaked, risk carried — in the buyer's own units before naming a price.
- **Show the delta.** Contrast the cost of the status quo against the cost with
  the product. The gap is the value.
- **Express it as a ratio and a payback.** "Pays for itself in 3 months, 5x in
  year one" travels further inside a buyer's organization than a raw dollar
  total.
- **Be conservative and sourced.** Build the model on the buyer's own inputs
  where possible; an inflated model dies the moment finance reviews it.

## Failure modes

- **Feature-led, not value-led.** The asset lists what the product *is*
  (features, specs, capabilities) instead of what the buyer *gets* (outcomes,
  costs avoided). The fix: rewrite a feature line as the outcome it produces,
  demoting the feature to evidence beneath it.
- **No proof.** Claims float without a number, a named customer, or a third-party
  signal. The fix: attach evidence to a claim, or cut the claim.
- **Generic battlecards.** One card meant to cover all rivals, naming none and
  beating none. The fix: one card per named competitor, each with real proof and
  honest gaps.
- **Discount reflex.** A price concession offered the instant cost is mentioned,
  training the buyer to push. The fix: reframe to value before any number on
  price moves.
- **Objection improvised live.** A predictable concern met with an answer
  invented under pressure. The fix: pre-script the five common objections through
  acknowledge → reframe → evidence → confirm.

## Red flags

- The headline names the product, not the buyer's outcome.
- A logo wall with no metric anywhere near it.
- A deck that opens on company history instead of the buyer's changed world.
- A battlecard with no competitor named in it.
- A testimonial with no number and no attributed source.
- A price stated with no value placed opposite it.
- An objection response with no confirming question at the end.
- "Trusted by industry leaders" and similar claims carrying zero evidence.

## Worked example — objection: "it's too expensive"

A weak response collapses into a discount: "I hear you, I can probably do 15%
off." A discount trains the buyer to push harder and concedes that the price was
inflated to begin with. Run the framework instead.

> **Acknowledge:** "That is a fair thing to weigh — budget has to clear a real
> bar, and I would ask the same."
>
> **Reframe:** "The number that usually matters more than our price is the cost
> of the problem sitting unsolved. Right now the manual workaround is consuming
> roughly 20 hours a week across the team."
>
> **Evidence:** "A customer your size was in the same spot. After switching they
> recovered about 18 of those hours weekly, and the platform paid for itself
> inside the first quarter — call it a 4x return over the first year on their own
> numbers."
>
> **Confirm:** "If we built that same model on your actual hours and it cleared a
> 3x return, would the investment make sense to bring to your budget holder?"

The reframe moves the conversation off the sticker price and onto value. The
evidence is specific, sourced, and from a comparable buyer, so it transfers. The
confirming question reopens the path forward and hands the champion the exact
artifact — a sourced ROI model — needed to win budget sign-off internally.
